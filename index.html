What Will It Really Take

Rethinking Women's Livelihood Programmes in India
Anaha Pandey | 2026

Section 1: The promise and the paradox

India has never invested more in women's economic futures than it is investing right now.

Over Rs 1 lakh crore was committed by nine Indian states in 2024 toward direct cash transfers for women. More than 90 lakh self-help groups cover an estimated 10 crore rural women across the country. The graduation approach, a carefully sequenced model that combines asset transfers, skills training, consumption support, financial inclusion, and coaching, has been validated across seven countries and sixteen years of rigorous evaluation. In India, every rupee spent has returned Rs 4.33 in long-term benefits, with gains in income, assets, food security, and health persisting years after programme exit. Nearly 75% of houses built under the Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana are registered in women's names, making it the largest transfer of productive assets to women in India's history. Women's participation in the rural employment guarantee scheme touched a decade-high of 58.9% in 2023-24. Women-led micro, small and medium enterprises nearly doubled to 1.92 crore in 2023-24.

By the numbers, the story looks like progress. And in many ways, it genuinely is. The organisations working in this space are serious, evidence-driven, and deeply committed. The programmes are not failing for lack of effort or intention. This paper does not question that commitment. It questions the architecture underneath it.

India's female labour force participation rate stood at 41.7% in 2023-24 according to the Periodic Labour Force Survey, a recovery from a historic low of 23.3% in 2017-18, but one driven primarily by rural women entering unpaid family labour and distress-driven self-employment rather than salaried work or enterprise of their own choosing. Women spend 299 minutes per day on unpaid domestic and care work compared to 97 minutes for men, a ratio of 3:1 that is among the highest globally and one that has barely moved in two decades. Only 35% of women with Jan Dhan accounts actively use them compared to 47% of men. Only 21% of women meet the Reserve Bank of India's minimum threshold for financial literacy. India ranks 131 out of 148 countries on the World Economic Forum's Global Gender Gap Index 2025 with a parity score of 64.1%. The NITI Aayog SDG India Index 2023-24 shows that SDG 5, gender equality, is the only goal where India's national composite score falls below 50, placing it in the Aspirant category.

The gap between programme scale and population-level outcomes demands an explanation. The standard explanations are well documented and real: delivery systems are slow and documentation requirements are burdensome, infrastructure is inadequate with women in remote areas travelling hours to reach a bank or market, market access is structurally biased with women constituting nearly 80% of the agricultural workforce but systematically underrepresented in governance of collectives, climate shocks hit women disproportionately, and the care economy remains the single largest time constraint on women's economic participation while India spends less than 1% of GDP on care infrastructure.

This paper does not claim to have discovered any of these barriers. What it argues is that the way they are currently addressed contains a structural problem that has not been examined with sufficient rigour. The dominant model of women's economic empowerment in India, whether through self-help groups, the graduation approach, skilling programmes, or financial inclusion initiatives, is designed as a set of individual interventions, each addressing one barrier, each measured on its own terms, and each largely unaware of whether the others are working.

A woman in rural Bihar can receive a productive asset through the graduation approach, a savings account through the Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana, financial literacy training from a Reserve Bank of India Financial Literacy Centre, agricultural training from the Mahila Kisan Sashaktikaran Pariyojana, and wage employment through the rural employment guarantee scheme, and still have zero independent income. Not because any single programme failed, but because none of them was designed to work in sequence with the others, none of them measured whether she retained control of what they gave her, and none of them checked what happened after they left.

The graduation approach measures whether a household crossed a consumption threshold but does not measure whether the woman within that household makes financial decisions independently. Self-help groups measure savings linked and loans disbursed but do not measure who controls the money once it reaches the household. Financial literacy programmes measure knowledge but do not measure whether a woman who understood the session could act on it without her husband's permission. India's rural employment guarantee legislation mandates creche facilities at worksites by law, but in most states that mandate is near-absent in practice.

Research from 20 randomised evaluations across 16 countries shows that removing any single component from a multifaceted livelihood programme reduces its overall impact. The components work as a system, not as independent interventions. Yet Indian policy continues to fund them independently, measure them independently, and deploy them without coordination.

Meanwhile, the evidence on what does work is unambiguous and underused. Direct deposit of wages into a woman's own account produced 25% higher earnings and 60% higher bank balances in Madhya Pradesh without any behaviour change from men. Mandatory property registration in women's names under the Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana transferred assets to 75% of beneficiary women without requiring any attitudinal shift. Automatic parametric insurance that pays out when temperatures cross a threshold reached 92% of insured women with zero claim process. These interventions work because the structural default guarantees the outcome regardless of household dynamics.

This paper makes one argument: women's livelihood programmes in India are designed to address individual barriers but not to guarantee sustained economic independence. They measure what is easy to count rather than what matters. The rest of this paper builds the case for why this gap persists, what it costs, and what a different architecture looks like, drawing on the journeys of three women whose experiences represent the median outcome of the programmes that were designed to help them.

Section 2: What has been built, and where the architecture leaks

India's women's livelihood landscape is one of the most ambitious policy ecosystems in the world. Over three decades, successive governments, civil society organisations, and development institutions have built a layered set of interventions that together constitute a genuine attempt to address women's economic exclusion at scale. Understanding what each layer does and where each one specifically fails is the foundation for any honest conversation about what needs to change.

2.1 Savings and credit: Self-Help Groups and bank linkage

Self-Help Groups are the load-bearing infrastructure of India's rural women's economy. Over 90 lakh groups cover more than 10 crore women. The SHG-Bank Linkage Programme had over Rs 2.6 lakh crore in outstanding loans to 77.42 lakh credit-linked groups as of March 2024, with a repayment rate of approximately 96-97%.

The credit model has a specific limitation that the evidence has now made precise. A Stanford and International Initiative for Impact Evaluation study using data from India's National Rural Livelihoods Mission found that small SHG loans, those below the Community Investment Fund (CIF) threshold, a larger pool of capital provided by the National Rural Livelihoods Mission to mature SHG federations for on-lending to members, of roughly Rs 50,000 to Rs 1.5 lakh, have no measurable effect on women's decision-making or intra-household resource allocation. Only when loans reach sufficient scale does financial access shift household power dynamics. The majority of SHG loans remain below this threshold.

The management information system tracks savings linked, credit linked, and repayment rate. It does not track what the loan was used for, who decided how it was used, or whether the woman who took the loan controls the income it generated.

2.2 Ultra-poor livelihoods: The graduation approach

The graduation approach is the most comprehensively evaluated anti-poverty programme ever designed. A synthesis of 20 randomised evaluations across 16 countries by the Abdul Latif Jameel Poverty Action Lab found large and lasting impacts. In India, the programme returned Rs 4.33 for every rupee invested. In West Bengal, consumption increased by 22% and income by 38% at year ten.

The approach works because it sequences correctly: consumption support precedes asset transfer, a coach visits regularly building both technical skill and confidence, and financial inclusion is linked after consumption is stable. Two gaps remain consistent. The programme exits after 18 to 36 months with no systematic post-graduation tracking. And it does not address care burden: a woman who receives a productive asset still does 299 minutes of unpaid care work every day, and the programme adds enterprise work on top of this without addressing the underlying time constraint.

2.3 Women in agriculture: Mahila Kisan Sashaktikaran Pariyojana

MKSP, a sub-component of the National Rural Livelihoods Mission, has reached over 34 lakh women. It provides technical training in sustainable agriculture, natural farming, and soil management, and has produced women community best practitioners who then train others.

Two gaps undercut its impact. It addresses production but not markets: women learn to grow better but sell to the same middlemen at the same prices. And it does not address land ownership: only 13% of women in states like Uttar Pradesh and Odisha have legal documents for the land they farm. Without a land title, women cannot access crop insurance under the Pradhan Mantri Fasal Bima Yojana (PMFBY), a government scheme that insures farmers against crop loss from natural calamities, pests, and diseases, formal credit as a farmer, or government subsidies tied to land ownership.

2.4 Skilling and wage employment: Deen Dayal Upadhyaya Grameen Kaushalya Yojana

DDU-GKY has trained millions of rural youth in industry-relevant skills with a mandatory 33% women coverage requirement. It provides residential training, post-placement support, and career progression tracking.

The access barrier is the programme's fundamental design flaw. The training model requires three-month residential stays at centres typically located 20 to 40 kilometres from rural villages. A rural woman with young children and household responsibilities cannot leave her home for three months. Placement rates do not account for retention: women who are placed often leave within months due to care responsibilities or family pressure.

2.5 Financial inclusion: Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana, Direct Benefit Transfer, Unified Payments Interface, Business Correspondents

The Pradhan Mantri Jan Dhan Yojana (PMJDY), a national financial inclusion programme that provides zero-balance bank accounts with a RuPay debit card and overdraft facility to every unbanked household, has opened over 540 million accounts, with women holding more than 55% of them. The gender gap in account access has narrowed from 20% to 6% in a decade. India accounts for 48% of the world's real-time payment volume.

But only 35% of women actively use their accounts compared to 47% of men. Business Correspondent reliability is poor: agents earn low commissions, face poor connectivity, and drop out at high rates. A single failed transaction experience causes permanent disengagement. Only 35% of Indian women own smartphones, making UPI app-based solutions inaccessible for most women these programmes target. UPI 123PAY (a version of UPI designed for basic feature phones that works through missed calls and IVR menus without internet) on feature phones exists but awareness and training at the last mile are near-zero.

2.6 Financial literacy: RBI Financial Literacy Centres, Swadhaar, NABARD FLCs

Financial literacy programmes have improved women's knowledge of financial products and in some contexts improved savings behaviour. Swadhaar's microlearning model, using behavioural triggers in relatable storylines, is one of the more evidence-informed approaches.

A 2025 study found that the association between financial literacy and financial inclusion is driven primarily by men and by the upper two wealth quintiles. The women most targeted by financial literacy programmes benefit least. A woman who understands compound interest but cannot open an account without her husband's permission, cannot take a loan without a male co-signatory, and cannot spend her income without household approval has not been empowered by knowledge. Financial literacy is necessary but not sufficient for financial agency, and treating it as sufficient is the consistent design error.

2.7 Social protection: Rural employment guarantee, Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana, Ujjwala, Ayushman Bharat, PMJJBY, PMSBY

The Pradhan Mantri Awas Yojana (PMAY), a housing subsidy programme that provides financial assistance for building pucca houses for economically weaker and low-income families, has a mandatory women's ownership requirement has put 75% of rural houses into women's names. Ayushman Bharat Pradhan Mantri Jan Arogya Yojana (AB-PMJAY), a government health insurance scheme providing cashless hospital coverage of up to Rs 5 lakh per family per year for secondary and tertiary care, has provided coverage to over 50 crore people. Pradhan Mantri Ujjwala Yojana, which provides free LPG cooking gas connections to women in below-poverty-line households to replace solid fuel cooking, has delivered 10 crore connections. The Mahatma Gandhi National Rural Employment Guarantee Act, now replaced by the Viksit Bharat Guarantee for Rozgar and Ajeevika Mission (Gramin) Act 2025 with an increased guarantee of 125 days, saw women's participation touch 58.9% in 2023-24.

These schemes operate as disconnected silos: a woman enrolled in the employment guarantee is not automatically enrolled in Ayushman Bharat or PMJJBY. The creche mandate at worksites has existed since 2005 but implementation is near-zero outside Karnataka, which runs 3,892 functioning creche centres through its Koosina Mane programme. A 2025 working paper from the National Bureau of Economic Research found that the employment guarantee, by guaranteeing household income, reduced women's intra-household resource share by 9 percentage points, effectively reducing women's bargaining power within the households the programme was designed to help.

2.8 Gender and rights: Strengthening Women's Institutions for Agency and Empowerment, Gender Resource Centres

SWAYAM, operating across four states, has established over 5,000 Gender Resource Centres and developed 17 gender training modules covering land rights, inheritance, domestic violence, and financial rights. An independent evaluation found that implementation is uneven and deeply dependent on individual champions. When a committed officer drives the centre, it functions. When that person transfers, the centre becomes inactive. The training reaches programme staff more reliably than the women themselves.

The pattern across all eight

Across every category, the same three gaps appear. Programmes deliver specific outputs to women but do not guarantee that those outputs become lasting economic independence. Programmes operate in silos, with women receiving agricultural training, financial literacy, and market linkage from different institutions that do not coordinate even when they operate in the same village. And the care burden, mobility constraints, and household authority structures that determine whether any programme actually changes a woman's life are treated as background conditions rather than design requirements.

Section 3: The toolkit and the journeys

The preceding section examined eight programme categories and identified where each delivers and where it leaks. This section presents a framework for what lasting economic independence requires, then applies it to three women whose experiences make the gaps visible.

The framework identifies ten conditions that must be present for a livelihood programme to produce sustained outcomes. These are drawn from a synthesis of 20 randomised evaluations across 16 countries (J-PAL Policy Insight, updated December 2023), India-specific evaluations by the International Initiative for Impact Evaluation, and qualitative field research. They function as a system: removing any single condition reduces the impact of all the others. But the sequence, emphasis, and configuration must vary by the woman's context.

Two of the ten operate as continuous threads through every programme stage. The remaining eight are sequential, but their order depends on who the woman is.

3.1 The ten blocks

Block 01
Consumption stabilisation
She cannot build if she cannot eat
A time-bound direct transfer that lands in the woman's own account before any other intervention begins. The graduation approach's most important design insight, validated across all evaluations, is that consumption support must precede everything else. Without it, productive assets are sold for food within weeks. For women above the extreme poverty line, this takes the form of an emergency savings buffer or consumption credit that prevents short-term crises from derailing longer-term enterprise building.
Block 02
Market-matched asset transfer
Produce what the market actually wants
A productive asset chosen from an evidence-based menu that reflects real local demand rather than programme tradition. Field evidence from the International Initiative for Impact Evaluation's 2026 visits across Madhya Pradesh, Rajasthan, and Uttar Pradesh found that tailoring accounts for one-third of women-led enterprises, followed by general stores at 30%, both of which are saturated, low-growth activities. This block requires a market assessment confirming demand before any asset is selected.
Block 03
Sustained community coaching
One-time training is just a conversation
A community-embedded coach who visits the woman in her own setting, speaks her language, and stays through the full programme. India's graduation implementation in Bihar deploys over 5,000 Master Resource Persons, each covering 30 to 35 households, with participant satisfaction exceeding 99% (SJY programme data, 2024). The coach builds technical competence and internal confidence simultaneously. One-time training programmes produce knowledge that does not survive the first real-world problem.
Block 04
Pre-production market access
She should not have to find her own customer
A buyer, off-take agreement, or aggregation platform identified before the woman begins production. An Observer Research Foundation analysis (April 2026) states directly: the national livelihoods architecture has historically focused on supply-side barriers rather than demand, creating a structural imbalance between producers and markets. Over 80% of women-led enterprises operate within household settings, constrained by mobility restrictions. The Self-Help Entrepreneur Mart (SHE-MART), announced in the Union Budget 2026 as a network of permanent retail outlets dedicated to selling products made by self-help group women, is the first structural attempt to fix this.
Block 05
Independent financial control
Access is not the same as control
Three things delivered together. Credit at sufficient scale: loans below approximately Rs 50,000 have no measurable effect on women's decision-making (Kochar et al., Stanford/3ie, 2022). Direct deposit to the woman's own account: the Madhya Pradesh direct deposit study (Field, Pande et al.) found 25% higher earnings and 60% higher bank balances. And financial literacy delivered after the account is active, through peers, in her language, with practical exercises. A 2025 study confirmed that the women most targeted by standalone financial literacy programmes benefit least.
Financial literacy is embedded here, not delivered as a standalone intervention. Peer-led, in local language, after account activation, using women who already use the product as the teachers.
Block 06
Care infrastructure
Five hours of care work do not disappear
Childcare and eldercare infrastructure co-located with programme activities, confirmed and functional before any training or enterprise activity is scheduled. Women in India spend 299 minutes daily on unpaid care compared to 97 for men (NSO Time Use Survey, 2024). India's rural employment legislation has mandated creche facilities at worksites since 2005. Karnataka is the only state to have operationalised this, through 3,892 functioning creche centres serving over 33,000 children (Koosina Mane programme, 2025).
Block 07
Bundled shock insurance
One shock should not erase six years
Automatic insurance bundled with every credit product, with payouts triggered by weather or satellite data directly to the woman's account, no claim process. Female-headed households lose 8% more income to heat stress and 3% more to floods (FAO Unjust Climate, 2024). Parametric insurance has reached 250,000 women across 34 districts in India with 92% payout rate and zero claim process (Climate Resilience for All / SEWA programme, 2025). The premium is under Rs 500 per woman per year.
Block 08
Aspiration and agency
She has to believe it is hers to have
Structured engagement that rebuilds the internal belief that a different economic future is possible. Research published in May 2026 by the Nudge Institute found that women in ultra-poor households do not lack aspirations; they lack the belief that their aspirations are legitimate. In Niger, the psychosocial component alone, without the asset transfer, still improved empowerment outcomes. The Strengthening Women's Institutions for Agency and Empowerment programme (SWAYAM) under DAY-NRLM has developed 17 gender modules and 5,000 Gender Resource Centres across 22 states, though implementation depends on sustained championship rather than episodic delivery.
Gender equity is embedded here: the belief that she has the right to own, decide, and earn, built through every coaching interaction, not through a separate module.
Block 09
Post-graduation continuity
Graduation is not the finish line
A pre-exit review asking five verifiable questions before graduation is declared: has she visited the bank independently in the last three months, does she know the price her product sold for, does she deposit income herself, is her insurance active, and is her independent income above zero. If any answer is no, graduation is deferred. Annual touchpoints for at least three years after exit, with automatic re-entry if income drops below a threshold. No programme currently does this.
Block 10
Women-level outcome measurement
Count what changes her life, not what is easy to count
Three intermediate outcomes tracked at the woman level from month one through three years post-exit, drawing on CGAP's 2024 women's economic empowerment measurement framework. Economic achievement: her independent income, tracked separately from household income. Resilience: her ability to recover from shocks. Agency: her independent financial decision-making, tracked through verifiable indicators such as whether she knows her bank balance and product price. This block enables every other block by making the invisible visible.

3.2 Three women

Champa, 29, Bihar
Women-headed household. Husband absent three years. Two children under six. No land, no savings, no bank account. Agricultural labourer, Rs 150-200 per day, roughly fifteen days a month. Over six hours daily unpaid care work, no creche in her hamlet. Identified as ultra-poor through Village Organisation wealth ranking. Entered the Satat Jeevikoparjan Yojana (Bihar's graduation approach). Chose pottery after market assessment confirmed local demand.
Sunita, 36, Madhya Pradesh
Husband Ramesh earns irregularly as daily wage labourer. Cultivates vegetables on 0.5 acres in Ramesh's name. Two school-age children. Two years in a self-help group, saves Rs 100 per month, at bank linkage threshold. Purdah practices restrict mobility. Financial decisions above Rs 500 require Ramesh's agreement. Two wings of the same national programme (SHG bank linkage and Mahila Kisan Sashaktikaran Pariyojana) operate in her village without coordinating. Strengthening Women's Institutions for Agency and Empowerment (SWAYAM) operates in her state but not her block.
Meena, 43, Rajasthan
Seven years in SHG. Three bank loans. Built a buffalo milk enterprise from one animal to twenty-seven. Household income tripled. Featured as a Lakhpati Didi (a government initiative recognising SHG women who earn over Rs 1 lakh per year as role models for rural women's economic progress) candidate. Husband Bhura manages all dairy sales and banking. Account in her name, PIN with Bhura. Has not visited the bank in two years. Rs 85,000 loan outstanding in her name alone. In 2024, Barmer's third consecutive drought year, two buffaloes died and she borrowed from a moneylender for the first time in six years. Last programme visit: sixteen months ago.

These are composite portraits drawn from field evidence, programme evaluations, and qualitative research. Their names are illustrative. Their situations are not.

A DDU-GKY skill training centre exists 25 kilometres away, offering three-month residential training. Sunita cannot leave her two children and mother-in-law for three months. The programme was designed for someone without care responsibilities and she is invisible to it.
09, absent
The SHG model has no graduation milestone or post-engagement outcome check. The programme either continues indefinitely with declining support or fades quietly. Sunita's independent income is zero. The management information system records her as a successful, active SHG member with a performing loan.
Land title is the binding structural constraint. Without land in her name: no crop insurance, no formal credit as farmer, no subsidy access. Requires policy reform beyond programme mandate, but the programme can design credit that does not require land as collateral.
Meena's actual journey (SHG, 7 years, Rajasthan)
Aspiration: Present during active SHG years, gave her identity and confidence. Eroded completely after programme withdrew at year 5. She credits Bhura publicly for the enterprise, survival strategy in Barmer's conservative hierarchy.
Measurement: Never tracked her independently. Household income used as proxy. Bhura's takeover invisible. She appeared successful because the household appeared successful.

Over two years of weekly meetings, savings discipline, and group solidarity, the SHG gave Meena a community identity beyond being Bhura's wife. This social capital is genuine and valuable.

The programme design failure happened at year 5, when the enterprise was large enough for Bhura to take over and vulnerable enough for one drought to collapse. Insurance, market access, and measurement should have been added at this transition point.

Across all three journeys, the blocks that are present vary by programme. The blocks that are absent form a consistent pattern: market access, care infrastructure, shock insurance, post-graduation continuity, and real measurement are missing for every woman regardless of which programme she is in. These are not implementation gaps that better execution would fix. They are design gaps that require a different set of interventions.

Section 4: What it takes

Section 3 identified where the ten blocks are missing or broken. This section describes the operational mechanisms that close those gaps, organised across five domains: programme design, technology, community ownership, financing, and pilot testing. Each mechanism is specific, costed, and testable. After introducing these mechanisms, this section shows what the three journeys look like when they are in place.

4.1 Programme design

Two assessments must precede any intervention. First, a market viability assessment: before any credit disbursement or asset transfer, the block-level team uses existing District Industries Centre and mandhi data to identify which livelihood activities have verified buyer demand. This narrows the asset menu to activities with real markets. Second, a time and constraint assessment: thirty minutes at enrollment to map the woman's available hours after care and domestic work, her mobility radius, and who controls financial decisions in her household. No programme currently does this.

The community organiser's KPI shifts from single-programme outputs (SHGs formed, accounts opened) to cross-programme coverage. For each woman, five binary conditions: does she have a productive activity with a verified buyer, does she independently control her account, does she have insurance, does she have care support, and has she received coaching in the last quarter. This requires a two-day orientation on every programme operating in the block, so the organiser can connect women to interventions beyond her own mandate.

4.2 Technology

Woman-initiated status signal (IVR). A missed call to a toll-free number triggers three questions in the local language via keypad. Has your enterprise income been regular? Have you visited the bank yourself? Did you face a shock you could not recover from? Responses aggregate at block level. A cluster of negative responses triggers a field visit. This generates women-level outcome data that no MIS currently captures, from the woman herself, not filtered through programme staff. Works on any phone including feature phones. Cost: Rs 8-12 crore per year for 1 crore women.

Real-time price transparency. Daily mandhi price SMS for the specific products women in that block produce, sent to the community organiser and shared at SHG meetings. This ends the information asymmetry that allows middlemen to underpay. The data exists in mandhi APIs. The SHG member databases exist. Connecting them costs approximately Rs 50 lakh to develop, Rs 1-2 per SMS to operate.

Automated insurance triggers. India Meteorological Department weather stations and Indian Space Research Organisation satellite data feed into a parametric insurance system. When rainfall drops below the insured threshold in a specific block, payouts go automatically to every insured woman's Jan Dhan account within 48 hours. No claim form, no field adjuster, no delay. The technology components are all operational. Connecting them is an integration challenge.

4.3 Community ownership

Community Enterprise Mentors. Women who have built and sustained enterprises for three or more years become peer coaches, recognised by the SHG federation. Each mentor supports five to eight women through monthly visits. She is not a programme employee; she is a federation member with credibility from her own journey. When the programme exits, she stays because she is part of the village. Cost: Rs 1,500-2,000 per month per mentor through federation funds.

Community-managed care. Three models through the SHG federation: rotating care where members take turns, a dedicated care worker employed by the federation during SHG and programme hours (Rs 3,000-5,000 per month), or co-location with the existing Anganwadi centre. None requires a new government scheme. Each requires the federation to treat care as a programme design requirement rather than each woman's private problem.

Federation-led post-exit monitoring. The Village Organisation conducts an annual review of each member: is her income regular, does she control it, has she faced a shock she could not recover from. Where answers indicate trouble, the federation activates its own resources first (emergency loans, mentor visits, scheme linkage) and escalates to the block office when community resources are insufficient.

Collective market access. SHG federations that operate as aggregation and sales platforms, receiving products from individual members, grading and packaging them, and selling in bulk, achieve prices 15-30% higher than individual sales to middlemen. Evidence from Andhra Pradesh and Kerala confirms this over more than a decade. The programme builds the federation's capacity during the active period. The federation operates the channel permanently.

4.4 Financing

Additional costs for all mechanisms:
Market and time assessment at intake: near-zero (existing data, 30 minutes added to enrollment).
Organiser 2-day orientation: absorbed in existing training budgets.
IVR status signal: Rs 8-12 crore/year for 1 crore women.
Price SMS: Rs 50 lakh development + Rs 1-2 per SMS.
Parametric insurance: Rs 300-500/woman/year. For 50 lakh CIF borrowers: Rs 150-250 crore annually.
Community Enterprise Mentors: Rs 900-1,200 crore/year for 5 lakh mentors (federation income + CSR + state mission).
Care arrangements: Rs 3,600-6,000 crore/year for 10 lakh clusters (already budgeted under employment guarantee creche mandate; enforcement and redirection, not new funding).

4.5 Pilot design

Geography: One block, one district, in a state with mature DAY-NRLM infrastructure (Bihar, MP, Odisha, or Jharkhand). Duration: 18 months (6 months setup + 12 months intervention). Intervention: All existing programmes continue unchanged. Seven additions only: market and constraint assessment at intake, organiser cross-programme orientation, IVR status signal, price SMS, one parametric insurance product bundled with CIF loans, community enterprise mentor cadre, and community-managed care. Control: Comparable block in same district, nothing changes. Measurement: Three indicators at baseline and month 12 for every SHG member in both blocks: her independent income (separate from household), whether she made a financial decision above Rs 5,000 independently, whether she experienced a shock she could not recover from. Cost: Rs 1.5-2 crore for one block over 18 months.

The design standard

The blueprint above is not a recommendation for one programme or three women. It is a design standard for any livelihood programme operating in India. Any organisation, government body, or funder can take the ten blocks and run their own programme through it as a diagnostic. Which blocks are present? Which are broken at delivery? Which are absent entirely? The answers will differ by programme and geography. The blocks will not.

The critical insight from the three journeys and the ecosystem mapping is that no new programmes need to be created to serve the ten blocks. The schemes exist. The institutional infrastructure of self-help group federations, Village Organisations, and Cluster Level Federations is in place. The financial inclusion architecture of Jan Dhan, Direct Benefit Transfer, and the Unified Payments Interface is operational. The legal mandates for care infrastructure and employment guarantees are on the books.

What is missing is the connective tissue: a community organiser who knows every programme in her block, a measurement system that tracks the woman rather than the programme, technology that puts price information and early warning signals into women's hands, community institutions that own the functions that programmes cannot sustain after exit, and insurance that protects gains instead of leaving them exposed to the first shock.

The cost of adding this connective tissue is marginal relative to the investment already flowing into each individual programme. The pilot design in Section 4.5 offers a way to test whether it produces different outcomes. If it does, the case for scaling rests on data rather than assertion. If it does not, the gaps are deeper than coordination and require more fundamental redesign of individual programmes.

Either result advances the conversation beyond where it currently sits: an ecosystem of well-designed programmes that cannot prove, at the level of the women they serve, whether they are collectively producing lasting change.
